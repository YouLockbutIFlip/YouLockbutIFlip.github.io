Do A Kick Flip: Exploring Unintended Vehicle Entry via OEM Sensor Attack Surface in Modern Tailgate Systems

Repositories Code Paper Demo Model ClipTool

Project Overview

Vehicle entry system security has been studied over the past decades, with most research focusing on communication and apps linked to the vehicle door lock control. The in-vehicle hardware components have typically not been considered as viable targets in attacks to gain vehicle entry, due to the requirement for tampering with the hardware/firmware or accessing the vehicle's internal interfaces such as on-board diagnostics ports. This work investigates the feasibility of non-invasively exploiting original equipment manufacturer (OEM) components in modern tailgate systems to achieve unintended vehicle access. Unlike commercial-of-the-shelf components, specifications of OEM modules such as kick sensors are often proprietary. Through electromagnetic (EM) injections and characterization of the module behaviors in lab settings, we discover that the modules generate subtle signals that allow adversarial probing and testing. Our experiments demonstrate that the modules can be triggered by transient EM signals with temporal patterns that mimic the motion and validate the attack by spoofing OEM tailgate kick sensors covering 12 vehicle brands. We then demonstrate adversarial tailgate operations on 4 real vehicles and perform end-to-end attacks on a 2023 Audi Q5 with an interactive, remotely controlled attack system. These results indicate that proprietary OEM modules with scarce public information can still be vulnerable to non-invasive manipulations, exposing an across-brand attack surface to affect the physical vehicle behavior like its lock operation, even though OEM vehicle components usually undergo electromagnetic-immunity testing (e.g., ISO 11452, SAE J1113).
